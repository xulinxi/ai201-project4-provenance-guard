# Provenance Guard — Planning & Specification

> Written **before** implementation. This document is both the design spec and the
> prompting tool used to drive AI-assisted code generation in Milestones 3–5.
> Edit history: stretch features (if any) are added below the line in
> [Stretch Features](#stretch-features) before they are built.

---

## 1. The Problem

Creative-sharing platforms (writing, music, art) increasingly need to answer a hard
question: *was this posted work made by the person who posted it, or generated by an AI
and passed off as human?* The goal is **not** to police creativity. It is to protect
**attribution**, build **trust**, and give readers the **context** they need.

Provenance Guard is a backend any such platform can plug into. It:

1. Accepts a piece of text-based content for attribution analysis.
2. Classifies it using **multiple independent signals**.
3. Produces a **confidence score that communicates uncertainty** instead of a forced binary.
4. Surfaces a plain-language **transparency label** to readers.
5. Lets a misclassified creator **appeal**.
6. Protects itself with **rate limiting** and a structured **audit log**.

### Design north star: false positives are the expensive error

On a writing platform, **labeling a human's work as AI-generated (a false positive) is
far worse than missing some AI text (a false negative).** A false positive insults a real
creator, damages trust, and can hurt their reputation. A false negative is a missed catch.

This asymmetry is not a footnote — it drives concrete decisions throughout this spec:

- The "Likely AI" threshold is set **higher** than the "Likely human" threshold (it takes
  more evidence to accuse than to clear). See [§4](#4-uncertainty-representation).
- When signals disagree, the system pulls toward **Uncertain**, never toward a confident accusation.
- The "Likely AI" label is worded as a *fallible automated estimate* and always offers an
  appeal path. The "Uncertain" label makes **no** AI-generation claim at all.

## 2. Architecture

### Architecture narrative

A submission's journey, naming every component it touches:

**Submission flow.** A platform sends `POST /submit` with the creator's `text` and a
`creator_id`. The **rate limiter** (Flask-Limiter, per-IP) first checks the caller hasn't
exceeded its quota. The request then enters the **detection pipeline**, which runs two
independent signals on the raw text: the **LLM signal** (`signals.llm_signal`) asks Groq's
`llama-3.3-70b-versatile` to judge how AI-like the writing reads and return a probability;
the **stylometric signal** (`signals.stylometric_signal`) computes structural statistics in
pure Python and returns its own probability. Both probabilities go to the **scorer**
(`scoring.combine_signals`), which produces a single `ai_likelihood`, then **`scoring.classify`**
maps that to an `attribution` (`likely_ai` / `likely_human` / `uncertain`) and a
`confidence`. The **label generator** (`scoring.generate_label`) turns that into one of three
plain-language transparency labels. The **audit log** (`audit.log_submission`, SQLite) writes
an immutable decision row and creates the content's mutable status record. Finally the endpoint
returns a JSON response containing the `content_id`, attribution, confidence, both signal
scores, and the label text.

**Appeal flow.** A creator who disputes a label sends `POST /appeal` with the `content_id`
from their submission response and free-text `creator_reasoning`. The system looks up the
content record, flips its **status** from `classified` to `under_review`, writes an **audit
log** entry of type `appeal` (carrying the original scores forward and attaching the reasoning),
and returns a confirmation. No automated re-classification happens; the appeal simply enters a
human reviewer's queue, which is surfaced through `GET /log` (and `GET /review-queue`).

## 3. Detection Signals

The system uses **two genuinely independent signals**. "Independent" means they measure
*different properties* of the text — one semantic, one structural — so their agreement is
informative and their disagreement is a real warning sign.

### Signal 1 — LLM semantic classification (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)

- **What it measures:** the holistic *feel* of the writing — semantic coherence, topic
  handling, voice, and the stylistic tells of AI prose (even-handed "on one hand / on the
  other," stock transitions, over-explaining, an absence of lived specificity).
- **Output:** the model is prompted to return strict JSON
  `{"ai_probability": 0.0–1.0, "reasoning": str, "indicators": [str, ...]}`.
  We use `temperature=0` and `response_format={"type":"json_object"}` for stable, parseable
  output. `ai_probability` is the signal's `P(AI)`.
- **Why this property differs between human and AI:** AI text optimizes for fluent,
  inoffensive, "balanced" prose and reaches for the same connective tissue repeatedly; human
  writing carries idiosyncratic voice, specific lived detail, and uneven emphasis.
- **Blind spot (must be stated honestly):** LLMs are *unreliable* AI detectors. They are
  biased against **non-native English** and **formal/academic** writing (correct-but-stilted
  prose reads "AI" to them), they can be fooled by **lightly edited AI text**, and they can be
  **prompt-gamed**. They also can sound confident while being wrong. This is the single
  biggest false-positive risk in the system and a primary reason the appeal path exists.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python, no external libraries)

A composite of measurable structural properties, each mapped to a 0–1 "AI-ness" contribution
and combined into one `stylo_score = P(AI)`:

1. **Sentence-length burstiness** (coefficient of variation of sentence lengths). Human
   writing mixes short punchy sentences with long winding ones (high variance); AI tends to
   uniform, medium-length sentences (low variance). *Low burstiness → more AI-like.*
2. **Type-token ratio (lexical diversity)** — unique words ÷ total words. Captures vocabulary
   richness/repetition. Used as a feature (with a length caveat below).
3. **Punctuation & informality markers** — density and variety of punctuation plus explicit
   *human-casual* signals (lowercase sentence starts, contractions, slang, ALL-CAPS emphasis,
   "…", multiple "!"/"?"). Presence of these strongly indicates casual human writing. *More
   informality → more human-like.*
4. **AI-tell phrase density** — frequency of stock AI connective/templating phrases
   ("furthermore," "moreover," "it is important to note," "in conclusion," "delve,"
   "navigate the complexities," "tapestry," "plays a crucial role"). *Higher → more AI-like.*

- **Output:** `stylo_score ∈ [0,1]` plus a `stylometric_detail` dict exposing every sub-metric
  (for transparency in the audit log and README).
- **Why these properties differ:** they capture *statistical uniformity* independent of
  meaning. AI text is structurally smooth; human text is structurally bumpy.
- **Blind spot:** **short texts** (< ~3 sentences) make variance and TTR statistically
  meaningless — the numbers swing on noise. **Stylized human writing** (formal essays,
  repetition-driven poetry, lists) looks "uniform" and scores AI-ish. A savvy user can
  deliberately vary sentence length to defeat it. **Genre confounds** it: technical/academic
  human prose trips the same wires as AI.

### Why the pairing is strong

One signal reads *meaning*, the other counts *structure*. Their failure modes are different:
the LLM is fooled by edited AI text but understands creativity; the stylometrics are fooled by
formal prose but are not gameable by semantics. Combining them is strictly more informative
than either alone — **and their disagreement is itself a signal** that we should be uncertain.

### How they combine → one confidence score

```
raw          = 0.65 * llm_score + 0.35 * stylo_score      # LLM weighted higher (more capable)
disagreement = abs(llm_score - stylo_score)               # 0 = perfect agreement, 1 = total conflict
ai_likelihood = 0.5 + (raw - 0.5) * (1 - 0.5 * disagreement)   # shrink toward 0.5 when signals conflict
ai_likelihood = round(clamp(ai_likelihood, 0, 1), 3)
```

The **shrink-toward-0.5** term is the key uncertainty mechanism: if the LLM screams "AI"
(0.95) but stylometrics says "human" (0.20), `disagreement` is large, and the combined score
is pulled back toward the uncertain middle instead of confidently accusing.

## 4. Uncertainty Representation

### What the numbers mean

- **`ai_likelihood` ∈ [0,1]** is the canonical score: the system's estimated probability the
  text is AI-generated, after combining and disagreement-adjustment.
- **`confidence` ∈ [0,1]** = `round(abs(ai_likelihood − 0.5) * 2, 2)` — the system's certainty
  in *whichever direction it leans*. `0.0` means a dead coin-flip (maximally uncertain);
  `1.0` means maximally certain. It is **monotonic** and **symmetric**, so a strong human
  verdict and a strong AI verdict can both report high confidence, and anything near the
  middle reports low confidence.

**What does `ai_likelihood = 0.6` mean to this system?** It means "leaning AI, but not enough
to say so." It falls inside the Uncertain band (below), produces `confidence = 0.20`, and
yields the **Uncertain** label that makes *no* AI claim. That is deliberate: 0.6 is exactly
the kind of soft lean where a confident accusation would be irresponsible.

### Mapping raw signals → calibrated score → label (asymmetric thresholds)

| `ai_likelihood` | attribution | label variant | confidence (example) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **≥ 0.72** | `likely_ai` | **High-confidence AI** | 0.44 – 1.00 |
| **≤ 0.35** | `likely_human` | **High-confidence human** | 0.30 – 1.00 |
| 0.35 < x < 0.72 | `uncertain` | **Uncertain** | 0.00 – 0.44 |

The thresholds are **intentionally asymmetric** (the false-positive north star in action):

- To be labeled **AI** requires `ai_likelihood ≥ 0.72` — a high bar, because accusing a human
  is the expensive error.
- To be labeled **human** requires only `≤ 0.35` — the benefit of the doubt is cheap to give.
- The **Uncertain** band spans `0.35–0.72`. It is **wider above 0.5** (0.22) than below it
  (0.15), so genuinely borderline text — and content where the signals are merely *leaning*
  AI — lands in **Uncertain**, never in a false AI accusation.

### How calibration will be tested (Milestone 4)

Run the four deliberately chosen spec inputs (clear AI, clear human, formal-human borderline,
edited-AI borderline) and confirm:

- clear AI → `likely_ai`, high confidence;
- clear human → `likely_human`, high confidence;
- **formal human → `uncertain`, NOT `likely_ai`** (the false-positive guard working);
- edited AI → `uncertain`, low confidence (mid-range, honest).

If any score contradicts intuition, print `llm_score` and `stylo_score` separately to find the
misbehaving signal before trusting the combined number.

## 5. Transparency Label Design

Three variants, written for a **non-technical reader**. Exact text below; the label function
injects the live confidence percentage where shown.

### Variant A — High-confidence AI  (`likely_ai`)

```
🤖 Likely AI-generated
Our automated analysis suggests this text was probably produced with significant help
from an AI writing tool. This is an estimate from imperfect detection signals, not a
verdict on the author — detectors can and do get this wrong. Estimated confidence: {confidence}%.
If you wrote this yourself, you can appeal this label and a human will review it.
```

### Variant B — High-confidence human  (`likely_human`)

```
✍️ Likely human-written
Our automated analysis found no strong signs of AI generation. This text reads as
original human writing. This is an automated estimate, not a guarantee of authorship.
Estimated confidence: {confidence}%.
```

### Variant C — Uncertain  (`uncertain`)

```
❓ Authorship uncertain
Our automated analysis could not reliably determine whether this text was written by a
person or generated by AI — the signals we use disagree or are too weak to make a call.
We are telling you this honestly rather than guessing. No AI-generation claim is being
made about this work.
```

**Why these words.** Variant A never says "this is AI" as fact — it says "suggests,"
"estimate," "detectors get this wrong," and immediately offers the appeal. Variant C makes an
explicit *no-claim* so a creator is never tarred by ambiguity. Both reflect the false-positive
north star at the UX layer, where it actually reaches the creator.

## 6. Appeals Workflow

- **Who can appeal:** the creator of the content. In this project an appeal is keyed by
  `content_id`; in production this endpoint would be auth-gated to the content's `creator_id`.
- **What they provide:** `content_id` (from their `/submit` response) and free-text
  `creator_reasoning` explaining why they believe the label is wrong (e.g., "I wrote this from
  personal experience; I'm a non-native English speaker so my style reads formal").
- **What the system does on receipt:**
  1. Look up the content. If `content_id` is unknown → `404`.
  2. Update the content's **status**: `classified → under_review`.
  3. Write an **audit log** entry of type `appeal`, carrying the **original** attribution,
     confidence, and both signal scores forward, and attaching the reasoning + timestamp.
  4. Return a confirmation: `{content_id, status:"under_review", appeal_id, message,
     original_decision{...}}`.
  - **No automated re-classification** (not required, and re-running the same fallible signals
    would defeat the purpose of human review).
- **What a human reviewer sees** when they open the queue (`GET /review-queue` / `GET /log`):
  the content id and text, the original attribution + confidence + both individual signal
  scores (so they can see *which* signal drove the call), the creator's reasoning, and the
  timestamps of both the original decision and the appeal.

## 7. Anticipated Edge Cases (content the system will handle poorly)

1. **Repetition-driven, simple-vocabulary poetry.** A villanelle, a nursery rhyme, or a poem
   built on deliberate refrains has *low* lexical diversity and *uniform* line lengths —
   exactly the stylometric fingerprint of AI text. The structural signal will push toward AI
   even though the work is human art. The LLM signal *may* rescue it by recognizing
   creativity, but if both miss, this is a real false-positive risk. **Mitigations:** the
   asymmetric 0.72 threshold (a single AI-leaning signal usually isn't enough), the
   disagreement shrink, and the appeal path.

2. **Very short submissions (one or two sentences).** Burstiness and type-token ratio are
   statistically meaningless on tiny samples — they swing on noise, so the stylometric signal
   becomes unreliable and can land anywhere. **Mitigation:** the pipeline detects very short
   inputs and reduces the stylometric weight / leans the result toward Uncertain, and the
   disagreement mechanism naturally widens uncertainty when the noisy signal diverges from the
   LLM.

3. **(Bonus) Formal / non-native-English human prose.** Correct-but-stilted writing triggers
   *both* the LLM's documented anti-formal bias and the stylometrics' uniformity detector,
   making it the worst-case false positive. **Mitigation:** asymmetric thresholds keep most
   such text in **Uncertain**; the appeal example in the spec is literally this case.

### False-positive scenario, traced end to end

A non-native English speaker submits a carefully polished personal essay. The LLM signal
returns `0.78` ("reads formal/AI-like"); stylometrics returns `0.66` (uniform sentences, few
informality markers). `raw = 0.65·0.78 + 0.35·0.66 = 0.738`; `disagreement = 0.12`, so
`ai_likelihood = 0.5 + 0.238·(1−0.06) = 0.724` — just over the AI line, a borderline call. The
creator sees the **Likely AI-generated** label, which explicitly says detectors get this wrong
and offers an appeal. They `POST /appeal` with their reasoning; status flips to `under_review`,
the appeal is logged beside the original scores (a reviewer can see it was the *LLM* that drove
the call), and a human makes the final decision. The system never silently brands them — the
confidence wording, the appeal path, and the audit trail together contain the damage. *(This
scenario also motivated nudging the AI threshold up to 0.72 and considering an even-higher bar;
see the spec reflection in the README.)*

## Stretch Features

All four stretch features are implemented. Per the project rules, each is **designed here
before** it is built, and documented in the README afterward. They extend the validated core
without breaking it — the required two-signal behavior is preserved (see S1's abstention rule).

### S1 — Ensemble detection (3+ signals, documented weighting + abstention)

Adds a third independent signal and turns the scorer into a weighted ensemble that can *abstain*.

- **Signal 3 — repetition / redundancy (`signals.repetition_signal`)**, pure Python. Measures the
  text's *internal self-similarity*: repeated trigrams, repeated sentence openings, and bigram
  redundancy. Un-edited AI prose reuses phrasings and loops; humans repeat less mechanically.
  This is genuinely distinct from Signal 2's burstiness (sentence *length*) and TTR (*vocabulary*)
  — it captures *repetition of content/structure*.
- **Abstention (the key design choice).** Redundancy is only meaningful with enough text, and
  *low* repetition in a short passage is **not** evidence of human authorship. So the signal
  **abstains** when `word_count < 60` and only votes on longer content. This is why adding it does
  **not** disturb the validated short-text calibration in §4.
- **Documented weighting.** Participating signals are combined by weight — `llm 0.55`,
  `stylometric 0.30`, `repetition 0.15` — renormalized over whoever actually participated. When
  repetition abstains, the weights renormalize to ≈ `0.65 / 0.35`: the ensemble **gracefully
  degrades to the original two-signal system**. Disagreement (max−min spread across participating
  signals) still shrinks the result toward 0.5.

### S2 — Provenance certificate ("verified human" credential)

A creator earns a durable *verified human* badge through a live writing challenge.

- **Challenge** — `POST /verify-human/challenge {creator_id}` returns a random writing prompt, a
  unique one-time **`token`** the creator must include verbatim, and a `challenge_id`. The token
  defeats pre-generated/replayed text: it proves the writing happened *after* the live challenge.
- **Claim** — `POST /verify-human {creator_id, challenge_id, response_text}` grants the credential
  only if: the challenge is unused and belongs to the creator, the response contains the token, is
  ≥ 40 words, and the detector classifies it `likely_human`. On success a credential row
  (`credential_id`, `earned_at`) is stored.
- **Display** — once earned, every `/submit` response from that creator carries a
  `creator{verified_human:true, credential_id, earned_at}` block, and the transparency label gets
  a prepended badge: **"✅ Verified human creator."**
- **Honest limits** — this is demo-grade *proof of a live, human-passing writing act*, not
  identity verification.

### S3 — Analytics dashboard

- **`GET /analytics`** (JSON) aggregates the audit log + content store: total submissions;
  attribution breakdown (counts + %); **appeal rate** (appeals ÷ classifications) and appeals by
  original attribution (surfacing that appeals cluster on AI-flags — the false-positive signal);
  average confidence overall and per attribution; **average signal disagreement** (the additional
  metric — how often signals conflict); verified-human creator count; submissions per day.
- **`GET /dashboard`** renders a minimal self-contained HTML view of those metrics for the demo.

### S4 — Multi-modal support (image captions / alt-text)

- `/submit` accepts an optional **`content_type`** — `"text"` (default) or `"image_caption"`.
- For `image_caption`, Signal 1 uses a caption-tuned prompt (AI image descriptions have tells:
  "an image of…", exhaustive completeness, generic adjectives), while Signals 2–3 and the whole
  scoring / label / audit pipeline are reused unchanged. `content_type` is echoed in the response
  and stored in the audit log.
- This proves the pipeline generalizes to a second modality (the *description* of an image) by
  swapping only the semantic prompt.

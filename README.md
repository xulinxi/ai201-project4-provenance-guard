# Provenance Guard

A backend service any creative-sharing platform can plug into to estimate whether a piece
of submitted text was **written by a human** or **generated by AI** — and, just as
importantly, to say so **honestly when it cannot tell**. It classifies content with an ensemble of
independent signals, scores its confidence without forcing a binary, surfaces a plain-language
transparency label to readers, lets misclassified creators appeal, and protects itself with
rate limiting and a structured audit log. All four stretch features are also implemented — see
[Stretch features](#stretch-features).

> **Design north star:** on a writing platform, falsely labeling a human's work as AI (a false
> positive) is the *expensive* error. Every design decision below leans away from it — see
> [Confidence Scoring](#confidence-scoring) and [Known Limitations](#known-limitations).

The full design rationale lives in **[planning.md](./planning.md)** (written before any code).

**📹 Walkthrough video:** https://www.loom.com/share/9ae2d9e41b444b85983097f903eb0378 — a short tour of the system working end-to-end with a few design-decision call-outs.

## Architecture overview

**The path one submission takes, end to end:**

A platform calls `POST /submit` with the creator's `text` and `creator_id`. The request first
passes the **rate limiter** (per-IP). It then enters the **detection pipeline**, which runs two
*independent* signals: the **LLM signal** (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`) reads the text
*semantically* and returns a probability it is AI; the **stylometric signal** (pure Python)
measures *structural* statistics and returns its own probability. The **scorer** blends the two
into a single `ai_likelihood`, shrinking the result toward 0.5 when the signals disagree. A
**classifier** maps that to an `attribution` (`likely_ai` / `likely_human` / `uncertain`) and a
`confidence`, and the **label generator** turns it into one of three reader-facing transparency
labels. Every decision is written to a structured **SQLite audit log**, and the endpoint returns
the `content_id`, attribution, confidence, both signal scores, and the label.

When a creator disputes a label, `POST /appeal` (with the `content_id` and their reasoning)
flips the content's status to `under_review`, appends an immutable audit entry **beside** the
original decision, and returns a confirmation — entering a human reviewer's queue
(`GET /review-queue`). No automated re-classification; a person decides.

```
POST /submit {text, creator_id}
   │   (rate limiter: 10/min;100/day per IP)
   ├─► Signal 1  LLM  (Groq llama-3.3-70b)  → llm_score    = P(AI)   [semantic]
   ├─► Signal 2  Stylometrics (pure Python) → stylo_score  = P(AI)   [structural]
   │
   ├─► combine_signals:  raw = 0.65·llm + 0.35·stylo
   │                     shrink toward 0.5 by |llm − stylo|   → ai_likelihood
   ├─► classify:  ≥0.72 likely_ai | ≤0.35 likely_human | else uncertain
   │                                                          → attribution, confidence
   ├─► generate_label → 1 of 3 transparency variants
   ├─► audit_log (SQLite, append-only)  +  content row (mutable status)
   └─► JSON response

POST /appeal {content_id, creator_reasoning}
   └─► status → under_review → audit_log (beside original) → confirmation
```

(Full diagram with labeled data flows: [planning.md § Architecture](./planning.md#2-architecture).)

## Detection signals

The pipeline's core uses **two genuinely independent signals** — one reads *meaning*, the other
counts *structure* — plus an optional **third ensemble signal** (repetition; stretch
[S1](#stretch-features)) that joins in on longer text. Their failure modes differ, so their
agreement is corroborating and their disagreement is itself a reason to be uncertain.

### Signal 1 — LLM semantic classification (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)

- **What it measures:** the holistic *feel* of the writing — coherence, voice, and AI tells like
  even-handed "balanced" structure, stock transitions, over-explaining, and an absence of lived
  specificity. Returns strict JSON `{ai_probability, reasoning, indicators}` at `temperature=0`.
- **Why I chose it:** it captures semantics no formula can — it understands what the text *says*,
  not just how it's shaped. It is the more capable single detector, so it carries the larger
  weight (0.65).
- **What it misses (blind spot):** LLMs are unreliable detectors. They are biased against
  **formal and non-native-English** writing (correct-but-stilted prose reads "AI" to them), can
  be fooled by **lightly edited AI text**, and can sound confident while wrong. This is the
  system's biggest false-positive risk. I mitigate it *at the source* by instructing the model in
  its system prompt that **formality is not evidence of AI** — see `signals._LLM_SYSTEM_PROMPT`.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python, no dependencies)

A composite of four measurable properties, each mapped to a 0–1 AI-ness contribution and
weighted into one `stylo_score`:

| Sub-metric | What it captures | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| **Sentence-length burstiness** (coefficient of variation) | Humans vary sentence length (high CV); AI is uniform (low CV). Low CV → AI-like. | 0.40 |
| **AI-tell phrase density** | Frequency of stock connectives ("furthermore", "it is important to note", "delve", "paradigm shift"). Presence → AI. | 0.30 |
| **Informality markers** | Lowercase starts, contractions, slang, ALL-CAPS, "…". Presence → human; **absence is neutral**, never AI. | 0.20 |
| **Type-token ratio** | Lexical diversity (length-capped). Deliberately weak — TTR is ambiguous. | 0.10 |

- **Why I chose it:** it is *independent of meaning* and not gameable by semantics. It catches the
  structural uniformity the LLM may rationalize away, and it runs free and offline.
- **What it misses (blind spot):** **short texts** make variance/TTR statistically meaningless
  (the pipeline detects this — `reliable: false` — and damps the signal toward 0.5).
  **Stylized human writing** (formal essays, repetition-driven poetry) looks "uniform" and scores
  AI-ish. A savvy user can deliberately vary sentence length to defeat it.

> Two design choices specifically reduce false positives: absence of informality and absence of
> AI-phrases both map to a **neutral 0.5**, not to "AI" — so formal human prose is never pushed
> toward an AI verdict on those axes alone.

### Signal 3 — Repetition / redundancy *(stretch S1)*

A third pure-Python signal measuring the text's *internal self-similarity* (repeated trigrams,
repeated bigrams, repeated sentence openings). Un-edited AI prose loops and reuses phrasings.
It is distinct from Signal 2 (which measures sentence *length* and *vocabulary*), and it
**abstains on short text** (< 60 words) so it never disturbs the validated short-text calibration.
Full rationale and weighting in [Stretch features → S1](#stretch-features).

## Confidence scoring

### How the signals combine

```
raw           = 0.65 · llm_score + 0.35 · stylo_score        # LLM leads (more capable)
disagreement  = |llm_score − stylo_score|
ai_likelihood = 0.5 + (raw − 0.5) · (1 − 0.5 · disagreement) # shrink toward 0.5 on conflict
confidence    = max(ai_likelihood, 1 − ai_likelihood)        # probability of the leading class
```

The **disagreement shrink** is the core uncertainty mechanism: if the LLM says "AI" but the
stylometrics say "human", the combined score is pulled back toward the uncertain middle instead
of confidently accusing.

> **Ensemble note (stretch S1).** The formula above is the two-signal case. The actual scorer is
> a weighted ensemble (`llm 0.55 / stylometric 0.30 / repetition 0.15`) renormalized over whichever
> signals participate. On short text the repetition signal **abstains**, the weights renormalize to
> ≈ `0.65 / 0.35`, and the math reduces *exactly* to the formula above — which is why the
> calibration below is unchanged. On longer text the third signal joins in. See
> [Stretch features → S1](#stretch-features).

### Asymmetric thresholds (the false-positive guard)

| `ai_likelihood` | attribution | label |
|---|---|---|
| **≥ 0.72** | `likely_ai` | 🤖 Likely AI-generated |
| **≤ 0.35** | `likely_human` | ✍️ Likely human-written |
| 0.35 – 0.72 | `uncertain` | ❓ Authorship uncertain |

The thresholds are **intentionally asymmetric**: it takes strong evidence (≥ 0.72) to call
something AI, but only ≤ 0.35 to clear it as human. The `uncertain` band is **wider above 0.5**,
so borderline and merely-AI-leaning text lands in *Uncertain*, never in a false accusation.

### How I validated it's meaningful

`calibrate.py` runs four deliberately chosen inputs spanning the range. Actual output:

```
case                       llm  stylo  disagree  ai_like   conf  attribution
----------------------------------------------------------------------------
clear_AI                  0.80   0.67      0.13     0.74   0.74  likely_ai
clear_human               0.20   0.29      0.09     0.24   0.76  likely_human
borderline_formal_human   0.60   0.55      0.05     0.58   0.58  uncertain
borderline_edited_AI      0.40   0.51      0.11     0.44   0.56  uncertain
```

This is exactly the intended behavior: a wide spread between clear cases (0.74 vs 0.24), and —
critically — the **formal human essay lands in `uncertain`, not `likely_ai`**. The false-positive
guard works. The two borderline cases sit near the middle with low, honest confidence.

### Two example submissions (noticeably different confidence)

**High-confidence example** — clearly AI text → **74% confident, `likely_ai`:**

```json
{
  "attribution": "likely_ai",
  "confidence": 0.74,
  "ai_likelihood": 0.738,
  "signals": { "llm_score": 0.8, "stylometric_score": 0.669, "disagreement": 0.131 },
  "label": { "variant": "high_confidence_ai", "title": "🤖 Likely AI-generated" }
}
```

**Lower-confidence example** — lightly-edited-AI text → **56% confident, `uncertain`:**

```json
{
  "attribution": "uncertain",
  "confidence": 0.56,
  "ai_likelihood": 0.44,
  "signals": { "llm_score": 0.4, "stylometric_score": 0.51, "disagreement": 0.11 },
  "label": { "variant": "uncertain", "title": "❓ Authorship uncertain" }
}
```

Same system, same code path — the score genuinely varies with the input (0.74 vs 0.56) and
produces two *different* labels, not a binary flip at 0.5.

## Transparency label

Three variants, written for a non-technical reader. The two confident variants inject the live
confidence percentage; the uncertain variant deliberately shows **no** percentage and makes **no**
AI claim. Exact text (`scoring.generate_label`):

### Variant 1 — High-confidence AI  (`high_confidence_ai`)

> 🤖 **Likely AI-generated**
> Our automated analysis suggests this text was probably produced with significant help from an
> AI writing tool. This is an estimate from imperfect detection signals, not a verdict on the
> author — detectors can and do get this wrong. Estimated confidence: **{confidence}%**. If you
> wrote this yourself, you can appeal this label and a human will review it.

### Variant 2 — High-confidence human  (`high_confidence_human`)

> ✍️ **Likely human-written**
> Our automated analysis found no strong signs of AI generation. This text reads as original
> human writing. This is an automated estimate, not a guarantee of authorship. Estimated
> confidence: **{confidence}%**.

### Variant 3 — Uncertain  (`uncertain`)

> ❓ **Authorship uncertain**
> Our automated analysis could not reliably determine whether this text was written by a person
> or generated by AI — the signals we use disagree or are too weak to make a call. We are telling
> you this honestly rather than guessing. No AI-generation claim is being made about this work.

`{confidence}` is replaced at runtime (e.g. `74%`). All three were verified reachable by
submitting inputs that produce each attribution (verified live during testing).

**Why this wording:** Variant 1 never states AI as fact ("suggests", "estimate", "detectors get
this wrong") and immediately offers the appeal. Variant 3 makes an explicit *no-claim*, so a
creator is never tarred by ambiguity. The false-positive north star reaches the creator here, at
the UX layer.

## Appeals workflow

A creator who disputes a label calls `POST /appeal` with the `content_id` from their `/submit`
response and free-text `creator_reasoning`. The system:

1. Looks up the content (404 if `content_id` is unknown).
2. Flips its status `classified → under_review`.
3. Appends an **immutable** audit entry of type `appeal`, carrying the **original** attribution,
   confidence, and both signal scores forward, plus the reasoning and timestamp.
4. Returns a confirmation with an `appeal_id`.

No automated re-classification — re-running the same fallible signals would defeat the point of
human review. A reviewer opens `GET /review-queue` and sees the text, the original verdict, *which
signal drove it*, and the creator's argument.

**Live example** (a non-native speaker contesting an AI label):

```json
// POST /appeal  ->  HTTP 200
{
  "appeal_id": 4,
  "content_id": "5475b866-ee91-4254-8324-95a46ca8c975",
  "status": "under_review",
  "message": "Appeal received. This content is now under review by a human moderator.",
  "original_decision": {
    "attribution": "likely_ai", "confidence": 0.74, "ai_likelihood": 0.738,
    "llm_score": 0.8, "stylometric_score": 0.669,
    "classified_at": "2026-06-30T01:55:02.989Z"
  }
}
```

## Rate limiting

Implemented with **Flask-Limiter**, keyed per client IP (`get_remote_address`), in-memory storage.

| Endpoint | Limit | Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| `POST /submit` | **10 / minute; 100 / day** | A real creator checks their own work *occasionally*; 10/min is generous headroom for legitimate bursts (re-checking after edits, batch-checking a few poems) while stopping rapid floods. 100/day caps sustained automated abuse — a script probing the detector or exhausting the Groq quota blows past 100/day fast, but a human rarely posts that many pieces. The two tiers defend **fast floods** (minute) and **slow-drip scraping** (day) separately. |
| `POST /appeal` | **5 / minute; 30 / day** | Appeals are rarer than submissions — you only appeal a contested verdict. Tighter limits prevent appeal-spam from flooding the human review queue, while never constraining a legitimate creator who appeals one or two pieces. |

**Limitation:** IP-based limiting is coarse (shared NAT/office IPs are throttled together; an
adversary with many IPs is not fully stopped). Production would key on the authenticated account
*and* IP, with Redis storage shared across workers.

**Evidence** — 12 rapid `POST /submit` against the 10/min limit:

```
request 1  -> HTTP 200
request 2  -> HTTP 200
   …  (3–9 all 200)
request 10 -> HTTP 200
request 11 -> HTTP 429
request 12 -> HTTP 429
```

The `429` body returned to clients:

```
429 Too Many Requests
10 per 1 minute
```

(A 16-request *parallel* burst confirms the cap precisely: exactly 10 × `200`, 6 × `429`.)

## Audit log

Two SQLite tables (`audit.py`): an **append-only `audit_log`** (the immutable trail) and a
**mutable `content`** table whose `status` flips on appeal. Separating them means an appeal is
logged *beside* the original decision and never overwrites it.

Every event records: `timestamp`, `content_id`, `creator_id`, `event_type`
(`classified` | `appeal`), `attribution`, `confidence`, `ai_likelihood`, **both** individual
signal scores, `status`, and (for appeals) `appeal_reasoning`. Retrieve via `GET /log`.

**Live sample — 4 entries (3 classifications + 1 appeal), newest first:**

## Stretch features

All four stretch features are implemented. Each was designed in
[planning.md → Stretch Features](./planning.md#stretch-features) before building.

### S1 — Ensemble detection (3 signals, documented weighting + abstention)

**What:** a third detection signal and a true weighted ensemble. **How:**
`signals.repetition_signal` measures the text's *internal self-similarity* — repeated trigrams,
repeated bigrams, repeated sentence openings — because un-edited AI prose loops and reuses
phrasings. `scoring.combine_signals` now takes the **participating** signals and weights them
`llm 0.55 / stylometric 0.30 / repetition 0.15`, renormalized over whoever participated.

The key design choice is **abstention**: low repetition in a *short* passage is not evidence of
human authorship, so the signal abstains below 60 words. When it abstains, the weights renormalize
to ≈ `0.65 / 0.35` and the ensemble **reduces exactly to the validated two-signal system** — which
is why the calibration table above is unchanged.

Evidence — a long, deliberately repetitive passage makes the signal participate and *carry* the
verdict even though stylometrics was neutral:

```
attribution: likely_ai | ai_likelihood: 0.722
signals: llm=0.9  stylo=0.496  rep=0.92   (redundancy=0.425)
repetition participates: True
ensemble_weights (renormalized): {llm: 0.55, stylometric: 0.30, repetition: 0.15}
```

### S2 — Provenance certificate ("verified human" credential)

**What:** a creator earns a durable *verified human* badge through a live writing challenge.
**How:**

1. `POST /verify-human/challenge {creator_id}` issues a random writing prompt plus a **one-time
   token** the creator must include verbatim (the token proves the writing happened *after* the
   live challenge — defeating pre-generated text).
2. `POST /verify-human {creator_id, challenge_id, response_text}` grants the credential only if the
   challenge is unused and theirs, the response contains the token, is ≥ 40 words, and the detector
   classifies it `likely_human`.
3. Thereafter every `/submit` from that creator carries a `creator{verified_human:true, credential}`
   block and the label gains a **`creator_badge: "✅ Verified human creator"`**.

Evidence:

```
challenge token: PG-39358185
verify -> verified: True  (detector: likely_human, ai_likelihood 0.297)
credential: {credential_id: VH-5CC1D5DC4D1E, method: live_writing_challenge}
later /submit -> creator.verified_human: True ; label.creator_badge: "✅ Verified human creator"
```

It is honestly **demo-grade**: it proves a live, human-passing writing act, not real-world identity.

### S3 — Analytics dashboard

**What:** an aggregate view of detection patterns, appeal rates, and signal behavior.
**How:** `GET /analytics` (JSON, via `audit.get_analytics()`) and `GET /dashboard` (a minimal,
self-contained HTML view that fetches `/analytics`). Metrics: total submissions; attribution
breakdown (count + %); **appeal rate** and appeals by original attribution (appeals cluster on
AI-flags — the false-positive signal); average confidence; **average signal disagreement** (the
chosen extra metric — how often the signals conflict); verified-human creators; submissions per day.

Evidence (`GET /analytics`):

```json
{
  "total_submissions": 3,
  "attribution_breakdown": {
    "likely_ai":    {"count": 1, "percent": 33.3},
    "likely_human": {"count": 0, "percent": 0.0},
    "uncertain":    {"count": 2, "percent": 66.7}
  },
  "appeal_rate": 0.0, "appeals": {"total": 0, "by_original_attribution": {}},
  "average_confidence": 0.663,
  "average_signal_disagreement": 0.241,
  "verified_human_creators": 1,
  "submissions_per_day": {"2026-06-30": 3}
}
```

### S4 — Multi-modal support (image captions / alt-text)

**What:** the pipeline handles a second content type — image descriptions — not just prose.
**How:** `/submit` accepts an optional `content_type` (`"text"` default, or `"image_caption"`).
For captions, **Signal 1 swaps to a caption-tuned prompt** (`signals._CAPTION_SYSTEM_PROMPT`) that
weighs caption-specific AI tells — formulaic openers ("an image of…"), exhaustive literal
completeness, generic adjectives — while Signals 2–3 and the entire scoring / label / audit
pipeline are reused unchanged. `content_type` is echoed in the response and stored in the audit log.

Evidence — an AI-style caption, scored with the caption prompt:

```
content_type: image_caption  ->  attribution: uncertain
llm reasoning: "The caption exhibits formulaic language and exhaustive literal
                completeness typical of AI-generated descriptions."
```

(Short single-sentence captions trip the short-text guard, so they often land `uncertain` rather
than a confident verdict — the correct conservative behavior for a low-information input.)

## Known limitations

The system will **misclassify** certain content. Honestly naming these is part of the engineering.

1. **Repetition-driven, simple-vocabulary poetry.** A villanelle, nursery rhyme, or refrain-heavy
   poem has *low lexical diversity* and *uniform line lengths* — the exact stylometric fingerprint
   of AI text. The burstiness sub-metric will push toward AI even though the work is human art.
   This is a direct consequence of Signal 2 measuring *structural uniformity*: deliberate poetic
   structure and machine uniformity look identical to it. Mitigations: the asymmetric 0.72
   threshold (one AI-leaning signal usually isn't enough), the disagreement shrink, and the appeal
   path — but a poem that fools *both* signals would be a genuine false positive.

2. **Formal / non-native-English human prose.** Correct-but-stilted writing triggers *both* the
   LLM's documented anti-formal bias and the stylometrics' uniformity detector — the worst case.
   In testing, a formal monetary-policy paragraph scored `ai_likelihood = 0.58`; the asymmetric
   threshold kept it in `uncertain` rather than `likely_ai`, but it came closer to a false
   positive than any human text should. This is why the appeal example is exactly this scenario.

3. **Very short submissions.** One or two sentences make sentence-length variance and type-token
   ratio statistically meaningless. The pipeline flags these (`reliable: false`) and damps the
   stylometric signal toward 0.5, but a short text is inherently low-information and will often,
   correctly, return `uncertain`.

## Spec reflection

**One way the spec (`planning.md`) helped.** Writing the confidence design *before* coding forced
me to decide what `0.6` should *mean to a user* before I could implement it. That up-front
decision — that a soft lean like 0.6 must read as *"not enough to say"* — is what produced the
asymmetric thresholds and the disagreement-shrink mechanism. Had I coded first, I'd almost
certainly have defaulted to a symmetric 0.5 split and only discovered the false-positive problem
when a formal human paragraph got flagged as AI. The spec turned a vague goal ("communicate
uncertainty") into concrete numbers (0.72 / 0.35) I could test against.

**One way the implementation diverged.** In `planning.md` I defined `confidence = |p − 0.5| · 2`
(distance from center, range [0,1]). When I built the label and ran the calibration, a clearly-AI
verdict reported only **48% confidence** — which reads as self-contradictory under a "Likely AI"
label. I switched to `confidence = max(p, 1−p)` (probability of the leading class, range
[0.5,1.0]), so the same verdict now reads **"74% likely AI"** — natural for a non-technical reader,
and a closer match to the project's own *"0.51 vs 0.95"* confidence framing. The lesson: the
confidence number is a UX artifact, and I couldn't tell my first definition read badly until I saw
it rendered inside the actual label. The divergence is documented in `scoring.py` at the
`classify()` definition.

## AI usage

I used an AI coding assistant throughout, directed by the sections of `planning.md`. Two specific
instances where I steered or overrode it:

1. **LLM signal prompt — overrode the default toward false-positive safety.** I asked the
   assistant to write `llm_signal()` to classify human-vs-AI and return a probability. Its first
   prompt treated formality and polish as AI indicators. I overrode this: a writing platform's
   worst error is flagging a real (often non-native or academic) writer as AI. I rewrote the
   system prompt to *explicitly* instruct the model that **"Formal, polished, or grammatically
   perfect writing is NOT by itself evidence of AI… Never raise your estimate for formality
   alone,"** and to return ~0.5 when evidence is weak. This pushed the false-positive defense down
   into the most subjective signal. (See `signals._LLM_SYSTEM_PROMPT`.)

2. **Scoring logic — corrected a silent drift to a symmetric split.** I provided the combination
   formula and asymmetric thresholds (0.72 / 0.35) and asked for `combine_signals` + `classify`.
   The generated code implemented a reasonable-looking but **symmetric** 0.5 cut and *omitted the
   disagreement-shrink term entirely* — exactly the "looks right, silently diverges from spec"
   failure the assignment warns about. I corrected it to the spec's asymmetric thresholds and added
   back the `0.5 + (raw − 0.5)·(1 − 0.5·disagreement)` shrink, then verified with `calibrate.py`
   that the formal-human case stayed out of `likely_ai`.

Beyond these, I revised the confidence definition (see [Spec reflection](#spec-reflection)) and
hand-checked every transparency-label string against `planning.md` rather than trusting generated
copy.
